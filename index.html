Introduction

Thermodynamics

First of all, in order to understand the basis of our project we have to talk about Thermodynamics. Thermodynamics is the process that affects the freezing and the melting of sea ice. For example, when the temperature of the ocean reaches the freezing point for salt water (which would be -1.8 degrees Celsius and 28.8 degrees for Fahrenheit), the ice begins to grow and when the temperature rises above the freezing point, the ice begins to melt. The amount and the rates for the growth and melting of the ice depend on how the heat is exchanged within the sea ice itself and between the top and bottom of the ice. Since we're tracking the growth and melting of sea ice, we are essentially tracking the thermodynamics of the sea ice.

Albedo and Positive Feedback Loop

When the ocean surface reaches freezing point, ice starts to form. As the months get colder, and less sunlight reaches the Arctic, additional sea ice forms, generally reaching its maximum in early March. Then once the temperature rises, and more sunlight reaches the Arctic, the sea ice begins to melt, typically reaching its minimum in early September. Thus the cycle continues of sea ice growing and melting, with the maximums and minimums occurring towards the end of the winter and summer seasons because the ocean lags behind the atmosphere in warming and cooling. As ice begins to grow, it starts to act as an insulator between the ocean and atmosphere. Albedo is the indicator of how well a surface reflects solar energy, and varies between 0 and 1, 0 meaning that the surface absorbs all of the incoming energy, while a surface with a value of 1 reflects that energy. Sea ice has higher albedo because of its grey-white surface, with a value of 0.5-0.7 on the albedo scale, compared to the ocean, which has an albedo of about 0.06. Sea ice with snow has an even higher albedo value, reflecting nearly 90% of solar energy. The larger the sea ice extent is, the more energy from sunlight gets reflected, which keeps the ocean and atmosphere cool. The thickness of the ice is another contributing factor in the regulation of water temperatures. The ice, known as multiyear ice, that survives the summer melt becomes thicker and stronger, making it much more effective at keeping the water and atmosphere cool. While, first year ice, ice that did not survive the summer melt, is much thinner and more susceptible to storms and heat. Each year as temperatures rise, there is less and less multiyear ice, and as a result the Arctic sea ice becomes less effective at keeping solar energy out. As the snow starts to melt, melt ponds form, which causes the albedo to drop, and more solar radiation is absorbed, which increases the rate of melting. As more sea ice begins to melt, the dark surface of the ocean becomes larger, and absorbs a significantly larger amount of sunlight, which then causes the sea surface and overlaying temperature to rise, which then leads to more ice melting, creating a vicious cycle known as the positive feedback loop or Arctic Amplification.

4. Explain how the analysis here along with global warming provides an example of this positive feedback look.

The ice reflects the energy from the sunlight (because the ice is white and has a higher albedo), but after it has melted there isn’t as much to reflect the light. There's more ocean surface and less ice, and since the ocean surface is dark it absorbs the heat. This absorption causes the surface temperature to rise, and this causes more and more ice to melt which makes it a large cycle of more ice melting. This positive feedback loop is reflected in the change between the 1980, 2012, and 2019 data where there is more ice overall, and the amount of sea ice decreases even if the rate at which the ice is melting is staying the same. Over the years more of the ice is melting and less is being frozen again once the months become colder, which can be seen in the initial graph of the data where 1980 clearly has more sea ice extent overall, even though it roughly follows the same path of melting. The 2012 and 2019 graphs also have more extreme contrasts between their maximum and minimums.
